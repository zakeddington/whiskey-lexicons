The Whiskey Lexicon

Geographical regulations · Whiskey varieties · Industry glossary

United States USA
Regulated by TTB (Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau)
▼
Core Legal Framework
Grain base: Whisky must be produced from a fermented mash of grain.
Distillation ceiling: Must be distilled to no more than 190° proof (95% ABV).
Entry proof: Must enter the barrel at no more than 125° proof (62.5% ABV).
Bottling minimum: Must be bottled at no less than 80° proof (40% ABV).
Oak requirement: Must be aged in oak containers (type varies by category).
Coloring/additives: No colorings, flavorings, or additives permitted in straight whiskey designations. Blended categories may allow limited additions.
Varieties within the United States
Bourbon
≥51% corn mash bill · New charred oak containers · Distilled ≤160° proof · Barreled ≤125° proof · Bottled ≥80° proof · No age statement if ≥4 years · No additives
Straight Bourbon
All bourbon rules plus: minimum 2 years aging · If under 4 years, age must be stated · No blending with other spirits · No additives
Tennessee Whiskey
Meets all bourbon criteria · Must be produced in Tennessee · Must use Lincoln County Process (maple charcoal filtering) before or during aging · Notable exception: Benjamin Prichard's is exempt from filtration requirement
Rye Whiskey
≥51% rye mash bill · New charred oak containers · Distilled ≤160° proof · Barreled ≤125° proof · Bottled ≥80° proof
Straight Rye
All rye rules plus: minimum 2 years aging · Age stated if under 4 years · No blending with other whisky types · No additives
Wheat Whiskey
≥51% wheat mash bill · New charred oak · Same proof limits as bourbon · Straight designation requires 2+ years
Malt Whiskey
≥51% malted barley · New charred oak · Same proof limits · Not required to use pot still · Straight designation requires 2+ years
Corn Whiskey
≥80% corn mash bill · Not required to be aged · If aged, must use used or uncharred new oak · Distilled ≤160° proof · No aging minimum
Bottled-in-Bond
Product of one distillation season · One distillery · Aged minimum 4 years in federally bonded warehouse · Bottled at exactly 100° proof · Label must identify distillery
Blended Whiskey
≥20% straight whiskey · Remainder may be unaged neutral grain spirit or light whiskey · May contain coloring/flavoring · Must be labeled "blended"
Light Whiskey
Distilled between 161°–189° proof · Aged in used or uncharred new oak · Light character, more neutral flavor profile
Scotland Scotch
Regulated by the Scotch Whisky Regulations 2009 (SWR 2009)
▼
Core Legal Framework
Production location: Must be entirely produced at a distillery in Scotland.
Water & cereal: Made from water and malted barley (for single malt) or whole grains of other cereals.
Yeast only: Only yeast may be added to convert starches to sugars; no enzymes.
Distillation ceiling: Must be distilled to fewer than 94.8% ABV to retain character of raw materials.
Aging minimum: Must mature in Scotland in oak casks not exceeding 700 litres for a minimum of 3 years.
Bottling minimum: Must be bottled at minimum 40% ABV.
Permitted additives: Only water and plain caramel coloring (E150a) may be added. No other additives.
Age statement: If stated, must reflect youngest whisky in the bottle.
Five Protected Scotch Whisky Regions
Highlands
Largest region · North of a line from Greenock to Dundee · Diverse styles from light floral to robust peaty · Includes sub-region Speyside · No single required flavor profile
Speyside
Officially a sub-region of Highlands but recognized independently · Centered on River Spey · Densest concentration of distilleries in Scotland · Typically fruity, sweet, and elegant · Includes Glenfiddich, Macallan, Glenlivet
Lowlands
South of the Highland line · Traditionally triple-distilled · Light, grassy, gentle character · Historically the grain whisky heartland
Islay
Island off west coast · Famous for heavily peated, maritime styles · High phenol content in many expressions · Includes Laphroaig, Ardbeg, Lagavulin
Campbeltown
Kintyre peninsula · Historically Scotland's whisky capital · Now only 3 distilleries operating · Briny, oily, slightly peated character · Springbank, Glen Scotia, Glengyle
Five Scotch Whisky Categories
Single Malt Scotch
100% malted barley · Batch distilled in pot stills · Single distillery · Aged min. 3 years in oak ≤700L · No blending with other distilleries' whisky
Single Grain Scotch
Produced at a single distillery · May include unmalted grains or cereals other than malted barley · Typically column-distilled · Lighter, more neutral character
Blended Malt Scotch
Formerly "vatted malt" · Combination of single malts from two or more distilleries · No grain whisky permitted · Must meet all Scotch regulations
Blended Grain Scotch
Combination of single grain whiskies from two or more distilleries · No malt whisky may be added · Relatively uncommon category
Blended Scotch Whisky
Combination of one or more single malts with one or more single grains · Largest category by volume · Age statement reflects youngest component · Examples: Johnnie Walker, Chivas Regal
Ireland Irish
Regulated by Irish Whiskey Technical File (2014) & EU Geographic Indication
▼
Core Legal Framework
Production location: Must be distilled and matured on the island of Ireland (Republic and Northern Ireland).
Grain base: Produced from a mash of malted cereals, with or without whole unmalted cereal grains.
Distillation ceiling: Distilled to less than 94.8% ABV to retain character of raw materials.
Aging minimum: Aged minimum 3 years in wooden casks on the island of Ireland.
Bottling minimum: Minimum 40% ABV.
Permitted additives: Only water and caramel coloring. No other flavorings or additives.
Irish Whiskey Categories
Single Malt Irish
100% malted barley · Single distillery · Pot still distillation only · Minimum 3 years aging · Typically unpeated (exceptions exist)
Single Pot Still Irish
Uniquely Irish category · Mix of malted and unmalted barley (minimum 30% each) · Pot still distillation only · Single distillery · Gives characteristic creamy, spicy character
Single Grain Irish
Single distillery · Cereals other than or in addition to malted barley · Typically column-distilled · Lighter, sweeter character
Blended Irish Whiskey
Combination of two or more of the above categories · Most widely sold Irish whiskey style · Examples: Jameson, Bushmills Black Bush, Tullamore D.E.W.
Canada Canadian
Regulated by Canada's Food and Drug Regulations (Division 2, B.02.020)
▼
Core Legal Framework
Production location: Must be mashed, distilled, and aged in Canada.
Grain base: Produced from cereal grains — no specific grain percentages mandated.
Aging minimum: Must be aged in small wood containers for not less than 3 years.
Bottling minimum: Minimum 40% ABV.
Permitted additives: May contain caramel coloring, caramel flavoring, and up to 9.09% of other Canadian or foreign spirits, wine, or other flavorings — a notably liberal rule compared to other regions.
Labeling note: "Rye whisky" is legally synonymous with "Canadian whisky" regardless of actual rye content; historically produced with high rye content but this is not required today.
Canadian Whisky Types
Canadian Whisky / Rye
The primary and general designation · No mash bill restrictions · Blending-focused tradition · Often light and smooth · Examples: Crown Royal, Canadian Club
Single Malt Canadian
Emerging category · 100% malted barley · Single distillery · Follows general Canadian aging rules · Growing craft distillery trend
Single Barrel Canadian
Non-legally defined marketing designation · Indicates whisky from one specific barrel · No regulatory standard — transparency relies on producer
Japan Japanese
Regulated by Japan Spirits & Liqueurs Makers Association Standards (2021)
▼
Core Legal Framework (2021 voluntary standards, effective April 2024)
Production location: Must be saccharified, fermented, distilled, matured, and bottled in Japan.
Water source: Must use water extracted in Japan.
Grain base: Malted grains must be used; other grains may also be used.
Distillation ceiling: Distilled to less than 95% ABV.
Aging minimum: Must be aged in wooden casks of ≤700 litres for at least 3 years in Japan.
Bottling minimum: Minimum 40% ABV.
Permitted additives: Caramel coloring only. No flavoring additives.
Historical context: Prior to 2021, there were no binding domestic regulations, allowing producers to import foreign whisky and bottle it as "Japanese whisky." The new standards closed this gap.
Japanese Whisky Categories
Single Malt Japanese
100% malted barley · Pot still only · Single distillery in Japan · Minimum 3 years aging · Often uses Mizunara (Japanese oak) or ex-sherry/bourbon casks
Blended Japanese
Combination of malt and grain whiskies · All components must be produced in Japan under new standards · Historically some included imported spirit
Grain Whisky (Japanese)
Made from grains other than or in addition to malted barley · Typically column-distilled · Used as blending component · Lighter, cleaner character
Other World Regions Global
India, Taiwan, Australia, and other emerging whisky nations
▼
India
Domestic regulation: India's FSSAI (Food Safety and Standards Authority) governs whisky production domestically. "Indian whisky" may legally be made from molasses-based neutral spirit blended with malt whisky — distinct from international definitions.
Export compliance: Products exported must meet the importing country's definitions. Indian single malts (e.g., Amrut, Paul John) are made from 100% malted barley, distilled in pot stills, and comply with international standards.
Age statement: No minimum mandated domestically; single malt exports typically state 3+ years.
Taiwan
Key producer: Kavalan (King Car Group) operates under Taiwan's Bureau of Standards, Metrology and Inspection (BSMI).
Climate accelerated aging: Taiwan's subtropical climate accelerates maturation significantly; 2–4 years can approximate 10–12 years in Scotland's cooler climate.
Mash bill: Primarily malted barley; production largely follows Scotch single malt methodology.
Australia
Regulation: Governed by the Australian New Zealand Food Standards Code and Food Standards Australia New Zealand (FSANZ).
Definition: Whisky must be produced from a fermented mash of grain, distilled to less than 94.8% ABV, and aged in wood for a minimum of 2 years.
Notable regions: Tasmania has emerged as a premium single malt hub; mainland producers use diverse local grains.
European Union
Spirit Drinks Regulation (2019/787): Whisky produced in EU member states must be made from a mash of malted cereals, distilled to below 94.8% ABV, aged minimum 3 years in oak casks ≤700L, and bottled at 40%+ ABV. Caramel coloring permitted.
Notable producers: Germany, France (Alsace), Belgium, Netherlands, Sweden, and Finland all produce under EU regulations. Scotch Whisky and Irish Whiskey have separate Geographic Indication protection.

American Varieties
▼
Bourbon
The flagship American whiskey. ≥51% corn, new charred oak barrel, ≤160° distillation proof, ≤125° entry proof, ≥80° bottling proof. No age minimum but must be "straight" if aged 2+ years. Can be made anywhere in the USA.
Kentucky Straight Bourbon
All bourbon and straight bourbon requirements plus: must be distilled in Kentucky and aged in Kentucky for minimum 1 year. Accounts for ~95% of global bourbon production.
Tennessee Whiskey
Bourbon criteria plus the Lincoln County Process (charcoal mellowing through sugar maple charcoal before or during aging) and must be produced in Tennessee. Jack Daniel's and George Dickel are primary examples.
Straight Rye Whiskey
≥51% rye grain. New charred oak. Minimum 2 years aging (age stated if under 4 years). Spicier, drier profile than bourbon. Maryland style historically lighter; Pennsylvania/Monongahela style heavier and oilier.
High-Rye Bourbon
Non-legal designation. Bourbon with a notably elevated rye content (typically 20–35%) in the secondary grain bill. Spicier character. Examples: Bulleit, Four Roses Single Barrel.
Wheated Bourbon
Non-legal designation. Bourbon using wheat as the secondary grain instead of rye. Softer, sweeter profile. Examples: W.L. Weller, Pappy Van Winkle, Maker's Mark.
Corn Whiskey
≥80% corn. If aged, must use used or uncharred new oak — the only major US whiskey not requiring new charred oak. Often unaged (white dog). Moonshine tradition.
Bottled-in-Bond Bourbon
Must be product of one distillation season, one distiller, one distillery; aged 4+ years in a federally bonded warehouse; bottled at exactly 100° proof. Government-backed authenticity guarantee since 1897 Bottled-in-Bond Act.
Single Barrel Bourbon
Non-legal designation indicating the bottling comes from one specific barrel, not blended. Each barrel differs. Requires age and barrel identification on label for premium tier.
Small Batch Bourbon
No legal definition. Marketing term implying a limited number of barrels blended (typically 10–200, but no minimum or maximum is set by law).
Cask Strength / Barrel Proof
Non-legal designation. Bourbon bottled directly from the barrel without dilution (or with minimal dilution). Typically 110–140° proof. Must still meet all bourbon requirements.
Scotch Varieties
▼
Single Malt Scotch Whisky
100% malted barley. Batch distilled in copper pot stills. Single distillery. Aged minimum 3 years in Scotland in oak casks ≤700L. No grain whisky blending. Each distillery's make is unique.
Blended Scotch Whisky
One or more single malts combined with one or more single grains. Largest category by volume. Age statement must reflect youngest component. Diageo and Pernod Ricard dominate this category.
Blended Malt (Vatted Malt)
Single malts from two or more Scottish distilleries blended together. No grain whisky included. Formerly called "vatted malt" or "pure malt" — terms now restricted by SWR 2009.
Single Grain Scotch
Produced at a single distillery from cereals other than or including malted barley, but not exclusively. Column distilled. Lighter character. Cameronbridge and Girvan are major producers.
Peated Scotch
Non-legally defined style. Malted barley kiln-dried over peat fires, imparting phenolic compounds. Measured in ppm (parts per million) of phenols. Islay is most famous but peat is used across regions.
Cask Finish Scotch
Non-legally defined term. Whisky that has completed primary maturation and is then transferred to a different cask type (sherry, port, wine, rum, etc.) for additional maturation. Duration varies by producer.
NAS (No Age Statement)
Non-legally restricted. Scotch whisky where the producer omits an age statement. Must still be 3+ years old. Allows flexibility in vatting different aged whiskies to maintain consistent flavor profiles.
Irish & Japanese Varieties
▼
Single Pot Still Irish
Ireland's most distinctive category. Mix of malted (≥30%) and unmalted barley (≥30%), distilled in pot stills at a single distillery. Creamy texture, green/grassy notes with a characteristic oiliness. Historically dominant before Prohibition killed export markets.
Irish Single Malt
100% malted barley, pot still distillation, single Irish distillery, 3+ years aging. Typically unpeated (Connemara is a notable peated exception). Often triple-distilled for smoothness.
Japanese Single Malt
100% malted barley, pot still, single Japanese distillery, 3+ years in Japan. Often uses diverse cask types including Mizunara oak, ex-sherry, ex-bourbon. Japanese distilleries often produce multiple still shapes to create diversity for internal blending.
Blended Japanese Whisky
Combination of malt and grain whiskies. Under 2021 standards, all components must be produced and aged in Japan. Suntory and Nikka are the dominant producers with cross-company barrel exchanges being rare (unlike Scotland).
Mizunara Expression
Non-legally defined designation. Whisky matured in Mizunara (Japanese oak, Quercus mongolica) casks. Very porous, difficult to cooperage, imparts sandalwood, incense, coconut notes. Rare and expensive.
